Life Skills Track · Grades 7–8

Cooking & Hygiene

Kitchen confidence, personal care, culture, safety, and dignified independence

Students learn practical routines for preparing food, caring for personal and shared spaces, and making informed choices. Lessons connect mathematics, literacy, science, Ghanaian and Akan foodways, the African diaspora, and New Orleans while protecting health, privacy, sensory needs, and individual dignity.

Learning goalsGrade 7Grade 8InstructionAssessmentIEP supportsReferences

Curriculum foundation

Useful and culturally grounded

Students practice real routines and investigate ingredients, tools, food histories, and household knowledge from Akan and Ghanaian communities, the African diaspora, New Orleans, and their own families without treating any tradition as a single universal way.

Safe before independent

Every task is explicitly taught, modeled, practiced, and checked. Heat, blades, allergens, chemicals, and food storage are governed by current safety guidance, staff supervision, and individual student plans.

Dignity and self-determination

Personal-care instruction is private, age-respectful, non-shaming, and responsive to disability, culture, gender, health needs, and student communication preferences.

Implementation standard: Licensed educators, food-safety guidance, families, occupational-therapy or related-service input when appropriate, and each student’s medical and IEP documentation guide implementation. This framework does not replace required health plans, allergy plans, or professional advice.

Enduring learning goals

01

Follow visual recipes, hygiene routines, and task sequences with increasing independence.

02

Use the food-safety sequence clean, separate, cook, and chill and explain why each step matters.

03

Measure ingredients, adjust quantities, read labels, estimate cost, and use time and temperature safely.

04

Plan meals and personal-care routines that respect nutrition, allergies, sensory preferences, culture, and available resources.

05

Care for clothing, tools, and shared spaces using safe products and organized routines.

06

Research and communicate how Akan, Ghanaian, diasporic, and New Orleans foodways carry history, adaptation, community, and creativity.

Grade 7 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Kitchen Readiness & Personal Hygiene

Essential question: What routines make a kitchen and a body ready for safe daily life?

Core learning: handwashing; grooming and dental-care sequences; privacy; kitchen zones; hazards; cleaning versus sanitizing; visual schedules; requesting help

Culminating demonstration
Complete a private, individualized hygiene routine plan and demonstrate a safe kitchen-opening checklist.
9 weeks

Tools, Measurement & No-Cook Preparation

Essential question: How do tools, measurements, and order of operations make recipes reliable?

Core learning: tool identification; cups and spoons; weight and volume; fractions; knife alternatives; cold-food safety; reading visual recipes

Culminating demonstration
Prepare and explain an accessible no-cook meal or snack using a visual recipe and a food-safety checklist.
9 weeks

Heat, Time & the Four Food-Safety Steps

Essential question: How can we use heat while preventing burns, cross-contamination, and foodborne illness?

Core learning: clean, separate, cook, chill; appliance routines; thermometers; timers; safe cooling and storage; adult-supervision signals

Culminating demonstration
Demonstrate a teacher-approved cooked recipe while narrating the safety decisions at each stage.
9 weeks

Foodways, Ingredients & a Shared Table

Essential question: How does food carry history, place, memory, and responsibility?

Core learning: plantain, yam, grains, legumes, groundnuts, spices, and local produce as research contexts; Akan and Ghanaian foodways; diaspora routes; New Orleans food history; respectful sourcing

Culminating demonstration
Create an illustrated ingredient story and contribute one safe role to a class shared-table simulation.
Grade 8 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Meal Planning for Real Needs

Essential question: How can a meal plan balance health guidance, preference, access, time, and cost?

Core learning: meal components; label reading; substitutions; dietary and sensory needs; planning lists; unit price; food waste reduction

Culminating demonstration
Develop a one-week fictional meal plan with a shopping list, substitutions, schedule, and stated accommodations.
9 weeks

Preparation, Storage & Recipe Adaptation

Essential question: How do skilled cooks adapt a process without losing safety or purpose?

Core learning: batching; scaling recipes; safe thawing and leftovers; flavor development; adaptive equipment; troubleshooting; documentation

Culminating demonstration
Adapt a teacher-approved recipe for serving size, equipment, or sensory access and justify every change.
9 weeks

Laundry, Personal Care & Shared-Space Stewardship

Essential question: How do private routines and shared responsibilities support health and belonging?

Core learning: laundry symbols; sorting; safe product use; room and bathroom routines; supply inventory; consent; boundaries; asking for support

Culminating demonstration
Complete a household-care rotation and create a private maintenance plan using the student’s preferred format.
9 weeks

Community Kitchen Capstone

Essential question: What does it take to plan and carry out a safe, welcoming food experience?

Core learning: menu; roles; workflow; accessibility; cost; food safety; communication; hospitality; reflection; waste plan

Culminating demonstration
Plan and operate a classroom café or demonstration using simulated money and teacher-approved food procedures.

Weekly instructional cycle

ConnectActivate prior knowledge, culture, purpose, and the student’s personal goal.
ModelTeacher demonstrates the complete process with clear thinking aloud.
PracticeStudents rehearse one step at a time with visuals, feedback, and repetition.
ApplyStudents use the skill in an authentic task, simulation, studio, or community context.
ReflectStudents document growth, revise work, and choose a practical next step.

Daily practice: Instruction may combine mini-lessons, visuals, movement, music, hands-on materials, role-play, small groups, assistive technology, independent work, and teacher conferencing. Pacing changes when student evidence shows a need for reteaching, more practice, or extension.

Signature learning experiences

Accessible visual recipe library

Students photograph or illustrate task steps, add safety cues, test the directions with a peer, and revise for clarity.

Akan–diaspora ingredient inquiry

Students trace one ingredient or preparation method through credible sources and distinguish documented history from family memory or assumption.

Private routine toolkit

Each student creates a discreet, personalized hygiene or household-care toolkit with icons, timers, checklists, or assistive technology.

Community café simulation

Students combine planning, measurement, budgeting, communication, hospitality, cleaning, and reflection in differentiated roles.

Assessment without high-stakes testing

Frequent evidence

  • Observation with a task-specific checklist
  • Student explanation, demonstration, or role-play
  • Portfolio artifacts, photographs, audio, or video when consented
  • Brief reflection and goal-setting conferences

Culminating evidence

  • Authentic unit demonstrations and projects
  • Application across more than one setting or format
  • Student self-assessment, revision, and explanation
  • Teacher rubric tied to the stated skill—not disability characteristics

Reporting progress

Progress reports identify what a student can do independently, with a prompt, with a model, or with direct assistance. Reports also name the next teachable step and the supports that improve access.

Reteaching and reassessment

Students receive a new explanation, adjusted materials, additional guided practice, and another reasonable opportunity to demonstrate growth.

Inclusive and IEP-aligned supports

Visual task analysis

One-step cards, photographs, color coding, first-then sequences, timers, and finished examples reduce working-memory demands.

Adaptive tools

Non-slip surfaces, adapted grips, lightweight tools, talking or large-display measures, and seated workstations support safe access.

Sensory planning

Students preview smells, textures, sounds, lighting, and temperatures and may use gloves, headphones, distance, alternate ingredients, or an alternate role.

Communication access

AAC choices, scripts, gesture, demonstration, and yes/no checks support requesting help, refusing contact, reporting danger, and explaining a process.

Errorless start, gradual release

Teachers begin with models and high support, then fade prompts only when performance is safe and stable.

Equivalent participation

A student who cannot safely perform one step may direct, measure, document, time, check, or explain without being excluded from the learning goal.

Individualization: Teachers follow each student’s IEP, present levels, accommodations, communication system, behavior or safety plans, assistive-technology needs, and specially designed instruction. Independence is never confused with withholding necessary support.

Health, privacy, and kitchen safeguards

The program treats safety and dignity as instructional requirements, not as rewards for independence.

Medical information controls the plan

Documented allergies, dietary restrictions, swallowing needs, medication effects, and medical protocols are reviewed before food activities. Staff never improvise around a health plan.

Personal care stays private

Students are never publicly compared, touched without consent, or shamed for odor, appearance, continence, motor skill, or support needs. Sensitive instruction occurs through approved, private procedures.

Hazards require authorization

Blades, heat, appliances, chemicals, and community food service are used only with approved equipment, trained supervision, and the level of support documented for the student.

Research and instructional reference points

Teachers use these sources as planning references, then adapt instruction to Aberewa students, local requirements, current safety guidance, and the expertise of qualified educators and community partners.

FoodSafety.gov: Four Steps to Food Safety

Federal guidance for clean, separate, cook, and chill routines.

FoodSafety.gov: Food Safety Charts

Current storage and safe-minimum-temperature reference charts for teacher planning.

CDC: Hand Hygiene in Schools

School-focused hand-hygiene routines, facilities, and visual cues.

The Met: Art of the Asante Kingdom

Historical and cultural context used alongside community expertise when studying Akan life and material culture.

Family and community connection

Students may interview a willing family or community member about a recipe, ingredient, household practice, adaptive strategy, or food memory; compare it with a researched source; and record what belongs to one family versus a larger tradition. An equivalent teacher-provided option is always available.

Privacy and choice: Family participation is welcomed but never required for a student to succeed. Students may use teacher-provided examples whenever sharing household, financial, health, cultural, or personal information would be uncomfortable or unsafe.
