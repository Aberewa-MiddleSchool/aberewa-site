Life Skills Track · Grades 7–8

Personal Finance

Money decisions, protection, planning, and community responsibility

Students build financial capability through realistic simulations, mathematics, reading, decision-making, and reflection. The course connects present-day money skills with family and community goals, New Orleans contexts, and carefully researched Akan histories of trade and measurement without presenting historical practices as modern financial advice.

Learning goalsGrade 7Grade 8InstructionAssessmentIEP supportsReferences

Curriculum foundation

Capability, not shame

Students examine choices, systems, tradeoffs, and protections without judging a family’s income, debt, benefits, banking status, or spending priorities.

Practice before real consequences

Budgets, pay statements, accounts, credit offers, taxes, insurance, and scams are taught with fictional data and simulations.

Values and community

Planning includes personal goals, mutual responsibility, generosity, sustainability, and the difference between helping others and being pressured or exploited.

Implementation standard: This is financial education, not individualized financial, legal, tax, benefits, investment, or credit advice. Teachers use current official sources, fictional data, and qualified partners when appropriate; they never collect students’ real account credentials, Social Security numbers, or family financial documents.

Enduring learning goals

01

Set a goal, distinguish needs from wants, and explain how values and circumstances influence money choices.

02

Read simplified income and pay information and calculate earnings, deductions, and take-home pay in simulations.

03

Create, monitor, revise, and explain a budget that includes saving and unexpected costs.

04

Compare banking and payment options by fees, access, safety, and consumer protections.

05

Evaluate prices, contracts, borrowing costs, advertising, and scams using evidence and mathematics.

06

Develop an ethical enterprise or community-resource plan with cost, revenue, risk, accessibility, and reflection.

Grade 7 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Money, Values & Goals

Essential question: How can money choices reflect a goal without ignoring real needs and limits?

Core learning: needs and wants; opportunity cost; short- and long-term goals; spending influences; saving; giving; privacy; decision routines

Culminating demonstration
Create a fictional goal plan with choices, tradeoffs, a timeline, and a method for tracking progress.
9 weeks

Earning, Work & Pay

Essential question: What information helps a worker understand earnings and take-home pay?

Core learning: hourly pay; salary concept; time sheets; gross and net pay; common deduction categories; workplace rights awareness; job accommodations; entrepreneurship

Culminating demonstration
Read a fictional pay statement, verify the mathematics, and explain where to ask questions.
9 weeks

Budgeting, Saving & Unexpected Costs

Essential question: How does a budget change when life does not follow the first plan?

Core learning: income and expense categories; fixed and variable costs; cash flow; emergency savings; sinking funds; revision; spreadsheet or envelope systems

Culminating demonstration
Manage a four-week fictional budget simulation that includes two unexpected events and a written revision.
9 weeks

Banking, Payments & Protecting Information

Essential question: How do access, fees, security, and convenience differ across financial tools?

Core learning: banks and credit unions; checking and savings; debit and prepaid cards; cash; direct deposit; fees; records; deposit insurance concept; account safety

Culminating demonstration
Compare three fictional account or payment options and recommend one for a stated profile using a decision matrix.
Grade 8 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Smart Consumer Decisions

Essential question: How can we compare total value instead of reacting to the first price or advertisement?

Core learning: unit price; quality; warranties; subscriptions; return policies; online shopping; advertising; reviews; contracts; needs and accessibility

Culminating demonstration
Complete a documented comparison purchase with total cost, evidence quality, risks, and final reasoning.
9 weeks

Credit, Debt & Interest

Essential question: What does borrowing make possible, and what can it cost over time?

Core learning: principal; interest; annual percentage rate concept; minimum payment; installment versus revolving credit; credit reports; cosigning; debt warning signs

Culminating demonstration
Compare fictional borrowing choices and use tables or a calculator to explain total repayment and risk.
9 weeks

Taxes, Insurance & Public Systems

Essential question: Why do people plan for costs that may be withheld, required, shared, or uncertain?

Core learning: sales and income tax concepts; W-2 and 1099 awareness; filing as an adult concept; insurance premiums, deductibles, and claims; public services; benefits privacy

Culminating demonstration
Build a fictional annual planning folder and explain which items require a trusted adult or qualified professional.
9 weeks

Enterprise & Financial Well-Being Capstone

Essential question: How can an enterprise serve people, cover costs, and act ethically?

Core learning: customer need; value proposition; startup and operating costs; pricing; revenue; profit and loss; recordkeeping; accessibility; community impact; risk

Culminating demonstration
Pitch a small fictional school or community enterprise with a budget, break-even reasoning, ethical safeguards, and reflection.

Weekly instructional cycle

ConnectActivate prior knowledge, culture, purpose, and the student’s personal goal.
ModelTeacher demonstrates the complete process with clear thinking aloud.
PracticeStudents rehearse one step at a time with visuals, feedback, and repetition.
ApplyStudents use the skill in an authentic task, simulation, studio, or community context.
ReflectStudents document growth, revise work, and choose a practical next step.

Daily practice: Instruction may combine mini-lessons, visuals, movement, music, hands-on materials, role-play, small groups, assistive technology, independent work, and teacher conferencing. Pacing changes when student evidence shows a need for reteaching, more practice, or extension.

Signature learning experiences

Money-journey portfolio

Students collect goals, decisions, revised budgets, comparison tools, and reflections showing how their reasoning changes over time.

Akan gold-weight inquiry

Students study credible museum evidence about historical Akan gold trade and weights as a context for measurement and exchange, clearly separating history from present-day currency.

Fraud-response clinic

Students identify pressure tactics, protect information, verify through an official channel, preserve evidence, and report a simulated scam.

Accessible enterprise pitch

Students design a product, service, or community project that includes cost, audience, accessibility, ethics, and a realistic operating plan.

Assessment without high-stakes testing

Frequent evidence

  • Observation with a task-specific checklist
  • Student explanation, demonstration, or role-play
  • Portfolio artifacts, photographs, audio, or video when consented
  • Brief reflection and goal-setting conferences

Culminating evidence

  • Authentic unit demonstrations and projects
  • Application across more than one setting or format
  • Student self-assessment, revision, and explanation
  • Teacher rubric tied to the stated skill—not disability characteristics

Reporting progress

Progress reports identify what a student can do independently, with a prompt, with a model, or with direct assistance. Reports also name the next teachable step and the supports that improve access.

Reteaching and reassessment

Students receive a new explanation, adjusted materials, additional guided practice, and another reasonable opportunity to demonstrate growth.

Inclusive and IEP-aligned supports

Concrete money models

Play money, number lines, labeled calculators, color-coded categories, and visual balance trackers make abstract amounts visible.

Reduced data load

Problems use fewer variables first, then add complexity through repeated scenarios and consistent forms.

Accessible documents

Simplified pay statements, contracts, bills, and account comparisons retain essential terms while using plain language, icons, and text-to-speech.

Decision routines

Stop–identify the goal–list choices–compare evidence–choose–check provides a repeatable structure for purchases and financial messages.

Private alternatives

All assignments can use fictional households. Students are never graded on disclosing real income, benefits, debt, banking, housing, or family choices.

Multiple demonstrations

Students may calculate, sort, role-play, dictate, use AAC, present a visual budget, or explain a decision conference-style.

Individualization: Teachers follow each student’s IEP, present levels, accommodations, communication system, behavior or safety plans, assistive-technology needs, and specially designed instruction. Independence is never confused with withholding necessary support.

Financial privacy and consumer safeguards

Financial instruction must protect students from exposure, pressure, and false certainty.

Fictional data by default

Teachers never request real account logins, card numbers, Social Security numbers, tax documents, credit reports, benefit notices, or family income.

No product promotion

Lessons compare features and protections rather than steering students toward a specific company, payment app, investment, lender, or fundraising platform.

Refer individualized questions

Questions involving taxes, benefits, debt, credit repair, investments, contracts, or legal rights are referred to a qualified professional or current official resource.

Research and instructional reference points

Teachers use these sources as planning references, then adapt instruction to Aberewa students, local requirements, current safety guidance, and the expertise of qualified educators and community partners.

FDIC Money Smart: Grades 6–8

Official middle-school lessons covering financial topics, learning objectives, real-life exercises, and family connections.

CFPB Youth Financial Education

Current federal tools for building financial habits, executive function, and financial knowledge and decision-making.

CFPB: Map Your Money Journey

A middle- and high-school activity for reflecting on current skills and planning growth.

The Met: Art of the Asante Kingdom

Museum context for historical Akan trade, leadership, and material culture used in the gold-weight inquiry.

Family and community connection

Families may discuss a goal, price comparison, household strategy, work experience, or consumer lesson without sharing amounts. Community partners may model banking, entrepreneurship, or fraud prevention only under school review and without marketing products to students.

Privacy and choice: Family participation is welcomed but never required for a student to succeed. Students may use teacher-provided examples whenever sharing household, financial, health, cultural, or personal information would be uncomfortable or unsafe.
