From Founder Vision to School Practice

Teaching & Student-Support Framework

Eight developing commitments translate the founder’s culturally responsive special-education manual into clear expectations for instruction, student support, family partnership, and professional practice.

Development-stage notice: This framework describes Aberewa’s intended direction. It will undergo educator, family, cultural, legal, accessibility, and governing-board review before becoming operative school policy.

Adapted from Aberewa Middle School: A Culturally Responsive Guide to Special Education by Antoine L. Mason Sr. The manual informs the vision; applicable law, professional standards, student data, and duly adopted policies will govern implementation.

Eight Instructional Commitments

01 · STRENGTH

Begin with capability

Educators identify interests, communication methods, successful conditions, cultural knowledge, and demonstrated abilities alongside disability-related needs. Support is designed to expand access and independence without reducing intellectual dignity.

02 · CULTURE

Teach culture with accuracy and context

Akan, Bono/Brong, African diasporic, and New Orleans knowledge will be connected to academic learning through named sources, historical context, community expertise, and respectful distinctions among peoples and traditions—not used as unexplained decoration.

03 · BELONGING

Design participation before correcting exclusion

Predictable routines, sensory-aware spaces, accessible communication, movement, visual supports, assistive technology, flexible grouping, and multiple classroom roles help students participate before difficulty is treated as defiance or inability.

04 · EVIDENCE

Measure growth in more than one way

Portfolios, projects, performances, conferences, observation, curriculum-based measures, student reflection, and standards-aligned rubrics may supplement required assessments. Accommodations and alternate response formats follow each student’s documented needs.

05 · PARTNERSHIP

Treat families as knowledgeable partners

Family communication will be accessible, respectful, and two-way. Families contribute knowledge about strengths, culture, communication, health, regulation, goals, and successful supports while retaining their full role in legally required educational decisions.

06 · REPAIR

Respond to behavior with safety, understanding, and accountability

Staff will use proactive supports, clear expectations, instruction, de-escalation, reflection, repair, and data-informed intervention. Restorative practice will not replace immediate safety action, required reporting, functional assessment, behavior plans, or disability protections.

07 · FUTURE

Connect learning to life beyond school

Communication, self-advocacy, daily living, technology, mobility, financial reasoning, relationships, community participation, and career awareness will be developed through age-appropriate instruction responsive to each learner’s goals and support needs.

08 · PRACTICE

Support educators as reflective professionals

Educators and paraprofessionals need induction, coaching, collaboration time, cultural learning, IEP and data training, assistive-technology practice, behavior-support preparation, and opportunities to examine whether instruction is producing meaningful student growth.

What Families Should See in Practice

Clear purpose

Lessons identify what students are learning, why it matters, and how success can be demonstrated.

Individualized access

Supports reflect the student’s IEP, communication system, present levels, strengths, accommodations, and current evidence.

Documented growth

Families receive understandable information about progress, support effectiveness, next steps, and opportunities to participate.

Legal and Professional Fidelity

Creative language is not legal paperwork. The manual includes original and fictional terms, sample frameworks, and illustrative IEP material. Aberewa will not substitute those terms or samples for official evaluations, IEPs, procedural safeguards, consent forms, service records, progress reports, behavior plans, or other documents required by law.

Actual student decisions will be individualized and completed by appropriately qualified teams using required forms, current data, family participation, professional standards, and applicable federal and Louisiana requirements.

How the Framework Will Become Policy

Professional review

Special educators, related-service professionals, administrators, legal counsel, and accessibility specialists review feasibility and compliance.

Community review

Families, neurodivergent people, Akan/Bono cultural advisers, and New Orleans partners identify strengths, concerns, and missing perspectives.

Board adoption and evaluation

The future governing board adopts policies, assigns responsibility, reviews implementation evidence, and approves revisions.
