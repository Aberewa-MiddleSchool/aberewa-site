Life Skills Track · Grades 7–8

Technology & Mobility

Accessible technology, safe movement, self-advocacy, and connected community life

Students learn to use digital tools, accessibility features, maps, communication systems, and supervised travel skills to participate more fully in school and community life. The course joins digital citizenship with New Orleans wayfinding, disability access, problem solving, and responsible use of cultural symbols and information.

Learning goalsGrade 7Grade 8InstructionAssessmentIEP supportsReferences

Curriculum foundation

Access is a right and a design goal

Students learn to identify barriers, select accessibility features, communicate support needs, and improve tools or environments rather than treating disability as the problem.

Safety is practiced, not assumed

Cybersecurity, pedestrian decisions, public transit, emergency communication, and community travel are explicitly modeled and rehearsed in simulations before any authorized real-world use.

Technology serves human purpose

Students select tools for learning, creation, communication, organization, and community contribution while protecting privacy and questioning whether a tool is accurate, fair, and useful.

Implementation standard: Real-world travel occurs only when authorized by the school, family, and student team, with trained supervision and individualized safety planning. Technology instruction follows school privacy rules and never requires students to disclose account credentials, precise home locations, or other sensitive information.

Enduring learning goals

01

Operate common devices, files, communication tools, and accessibility settings with an organized support system.

02

Protect accounts and personal information by recognizing scams, using strong unique passwords, multifactor authentication when available, and safe update practices.

03

Evaluate online information, digital media, and AI-assisted output for source, evidence, bias, purpose, and possible harm.

04

Read maps, signs, schedules, landmarks, and route information and communicate location or support needs.

05

Demonstrate safe pedestrian, passenger, and public-transit decisions in simulations and authorized practice.

06

Use technology and mobility skills to solve a meaningful school or community access problem.

Grade 7 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Devices, Files & Accessibility Features

Essential question: Which settings and routines help each learner use technology with less friction?

Core learning: device care; login boundaries; files and folders; keyboard and speech input; text size; contrast; captions; screen readers; reminders; troubleshooting scripts

Culminating demonstration
Build and demonstrate a personal access profile using fictional account information and approved device settings.
9 weeks

Digital Citizenship & Information Care

Essential question: What do we owe ourselves and other people when we communicate online?

Core learning: privacy; consent; respectful communication; digital footprint; source checking; copyright; AI disclosure; responding to cyberbullying; asking an adult for help

Culminating demonstration
Create an accessible digital-citizenship guide for a realistic middle-school scenario.
9 weeks

Wayfinding, Maps & Landmarks

Essential question: How can multiple kinds of information help us know where we are and where to go?

Core learning: cardinal directions; symbols; scale; street grids; landmarks; accessible entrances; route sequencing; location language; paper and digital maps

Culminating demonstration
Design an accessible route card between two approved school locations with landmarks, choices, and help points.
9 weeks

Safe Movement in School and Community

Essential question: How do observation, communication, and practiced routines reduce risk?

Core learning: hallways; crossings; parking lots; passenger behavior; weather; carrying identification according to policy; trusted helpers; emergency scripts

Culminating demonstration
Complete a supervised school-site mobility course and explain each safety decision.
Grade 8 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Productivity, Creation & Collaboration

Essential question: How can we choose the right tool and make our work usable by others?

Core learning: documents; calendars; shared work; captions; alt text; presentations; forms; file naming; version control; audience; accessible design

Culminating demonstration
Produce an accessible digital guide or tutorial that another student can successfully use.
9 weeks

Cybersecurity, Scams & Account Protection

Essential question: How can we recognize pressure, deception, and risky requests before acting?

Core learning: phishing; suspicious links; passwords; password managers; multifactor authentication; software updates; device loss; reporting; recovery steps

Culminating demonstration
Analyze a set of simulated messages and create a decision tree for stop, verify, report, or proceed.
9 weeks

New Orleans Transit & Supported Trip Planning

Essential question: What information and backup plans are needed for a safe public-transit trip?

Core learning: RTA modes; schedules; stops; fares as teacher-provided current data; accessibility; paratransit awareness; weather; transfers; delays; help-seeking; backup contacts

Culminating demonstration
Develop a fictional or team-approved trip plan with route, time, cost, accessibility checks, and two backup plans.
9 weeks

Mobility and Technology Capstone

Essential question: How can design improve access and participation?

Core learning: accessibility audit; interviews with consent; data; prototyping; user testing; revision; advocacy; presentation

Culminating demonstration
Propose and test one improvement to a school navigation, communication, or digital-access challenge.

PracticeStudents rehearse one step at a time with visuals, feedback, and repetition.
ApplyStudents use the skill in an authentic task, simulation, studio, or community context.

Daily practice: Instruction may combine mini-lessons, visuals, movement, music, hands-on materials, role-play, small groups, assistive technology, independent work, and teacher conferencing. Pacing changes when student evidence shows a need for reteaching, more practice, or extension.

Signature learning experiences

Personal accessibility profile

Students identify helpful features, communication preferences, environmental supports, and troubleshooting steps without publishing private disability information.

Cyber-safety campaign

Students turn realistic scam, privacy, and account-protection scenarios into clear posters, videos, role-plays, or AAC-accessible scripts.

New Orleans route portfolio

Students compare paper maps, landmarks, and current RTA information while documenting accessibility questions and backup choices.

Access-design challenge

Student teams observe a barrier, gather consented feedback, prototype a solution, test it, and present evidence for revision.

Assessment without high-stakes testing

Frequent evidence

  • Observation with a task-specific checklist
  • Student explanation, demonstration, or role-play
  • Portfolio artifacts, photographs, audio, or video when consented
  • Brief reflection and goal-setting conferences

Culminating evidence

  • Authentic unit demonstrations and projects
  • Application across more than one setting or format
  • Student self-assessment, revision, and explanation
  • Teacher rubric tied to the stated skill—not disability characteristics

Reporting progress

Progress reports identify what a student can do independently, with a prompt, with a model, or with direct assistance. Reports also name the next teachable step and the supports that improve access.

Reteaching and reassessment

Students receive a new explanation, adjusted materials, additional guided practice, and another reasonable opportunity to demonstrate growth.

Inclusive and IEP-aligned supports

Task strips and screen maps

Annotated screenshots, keyboard overlays, short videos, and numbered steps support memory and navigation.

Assistive technology choice

Speech input, switch access, AAC, screen readers, captions, text-to-speech, magnification, reminders, and low-tech alternatives are taught as valid tools.

Protected simulations

Students practice phishing, route decisions, money for fares, and emergency communication with fictional data before any live application.

Travel rehearsal

Social narratives, video modeling, miniature maps, role-play, repeated routes, and planned regulation breaks prepare students for authorized movement.

Communication backups

Printed cards, AAC messages, visual identifiers, and rehearsed help scripts provide alternatives when speech, a device, or a network is unavailable.

Flexible demonstration

Students may navigate, direct a partner, annotate a map, record a tutorial, operate a device, or explain decisions according to the target and IEP.

Individualization: Teachers follow each student’s IEP, present levels, accommodations, communication system, behavior or safety plans, assistive-technology needs, and specially designed instruction. Independence is never confused with withholding necessary support.

Digital, location, and travel safeguards

The course increases informed participation without promising or forcing independent travel for every student.

No credential or location collection

Practice uses fictional usernames, addresses, account numbers, and routes unless a specific authorized plan requires otherwise. Students never share passwords or precise home locations in class products.

Travel is team authorized

A classroom lesson or successful simulation does not authorize unsupervised travel. The student, family, school, and relevant IEP or safety team determine real-world practice and support.

Current local information is checked

Transit routes, fares, accessibility details, alerts, and emergency procedures can change. Teachers verify them directly with official sources immediately before instruction or travel.

Research and instructional reference points

Teachers use these sources as planning references, then adapt instruction to Aberewa students, local requirements, current safety guidance, and the expertise of qualified educators and community partners.

ISTE Standards for Students

Current framework for empowered learning, digital citizenship, knowledge construction, design, computation, communication, and collaboration.

CISA: Secure Yourself & Your Family

Current federal guidance on phishing, strong passwords, multifactor authentication, and software updates.

New Orleans RTA: Know Before You Go

Official current rider information for buses, streetcars, ferries, and trip preparation.

NHTSA: Pedestrian Safety

Federal safety resources, including safer-journey materials for young people.

Family and community connection

With consent, families and community partners may identify accessibility features, trusted routes, technology routines, and help-seeking strategies that already work. Students may also examine a fully fictional profile so no family must disclose travel, disability, or device information.

Privacy and choice: Family participation is welcomed but never required for a student to succeed. Students may use teacher-provided examples whenever sharing household, financial, health, cultural, or personal information would be uncomfortable or unsafe.
