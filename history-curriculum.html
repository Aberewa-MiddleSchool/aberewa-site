Academic Track · Grades 7–8

History

Power, evidence, memory, and the African world

Students investigate history as an evidence-based conversation. The program centers African and African diasporic experiences, Akan history, Indigenous perspectives, New Orleans, and the ways ordinary people respond to power and change.

Learning goalsGrade 7Grade 8InstructionAssessmentSupports

Curriculum foundation

Akan-rooted

Learning values community, memory, responsibility, wisdom, creativity, and relationships across generations.

Inclusive by design

Students receive explicit teaching, accessible materials, purposeful repetition, multiple representations, and multiple ways to demonstrate learning.

Restorative and humane

Reflection, dialogue, repair, collaboration, and growth replace fear-based discipline and unnecessary high-stakes testing.

Enduring learning goals

01

Build chronological and geographic understanding

02

Analyze primary and secondary sources

03

Distinguish evidence, interpretation, perspective, and misinformation

04

Explain cause, consequence, continuity, and change

05

Connect local, African, national, and global histories

06

Create ethical historical narratives that include multiple voices

Grade 7 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Historical Thinking & Ancient African Worlds

Essential question: How do we know what happened before us?

Core learning: Timelines; maps; archaeology; oral history; source analysis; early African societies

Culminating demonstration
Curate a mini-museum explaining how different sources reveal an African society.
9 weeks

Akan Societies, Trade & Governance

Essential question: How did Akan communities organize knowledge, leadership, and exchange?

Core learning: Akan history; gold trade; kinship; governance; symbols; comparison

Culminating demonstration
Create an evidence-based exhibit on Akan institutions, Adinkra meanings, and historical change.
9 weeks

Atlantic Worlds, Resistance & Survival

Essential question: How did people preserve humanity under systems of exploitation?

Core learning: Atlantic trade; enslavement; resistance; cultural retention; diaspora

Culminating demonstration
Map one cultural idea, practice, or form of resistance across the Atlantic world.
9 weeks

New Orleans: Place, People & Memory

Essential question: Who built New Orleans, and how should the city remember them?

Core learning: Indigenous history; colonialism; free people of color; migration; neighborhoods; public memory

Culminating demonstration
Design a historical walking-tour proposal with researched stops and accessible narration.
Grade 8 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Reconstruction, Freedom & the Unfinished Nation

Essential question: What does freedom require beyond a law?

Core learning: Civil War aftermath; Reconstruction; citizenship; Black political life; backlash

Culminating demonstration
Use primary sources to create a public explanation of Reconstruction’s achievements and limits.
9 weeks

Industrialization, Migration & Urban Life

Essential question: Why do people move, and how does movement transform communities?

Core learning: Labor; immigration; Great Migration; cities; technology; inequality

Culminating demonstration
Build a migration story map connecting national patterns to New Orleans.
9 weeks

Civil Rights, Black Power & Community Organizing

Essential question: How do communities organize for change?

Core learning: Movement strategies; local activism; youth leadership; media; policy

Culminating demonstration
Develop a documented case study of a movement, organization, or New Orleans campaign.
9 weeks

The Contemporary World & Civic Futures

Essential question: How should historical knowledge guide present choices?

Core learning: Global interdependence; voting; public policy; media literacy; civic action

Culminating demonstration
Present a civic-history capstone that traces a current issue through its historical roots.

Assessment without high-stakes testing

Frequent evidence

  • Conferences and observation
  • Exit responses and retrieval practice
  • Notebook, portfolio, or model checks
  • Short performances and explanations

Culminating evidence

  • Unit projects and demonstrations
  • Presentations, exhibitions, or performances
  • Student reflection and revision
  • Standards-aligned teacher rubrics

Reporting progress

Progress reports describe what a student understands, can do independently, can do with support, and should learn next. Grades never stand alone without feedback.

Reteaching and reassessment

Students receive another pathway to learn and another reasonable opportunity to demonstrate growth after feedback and practice.

Inclusive supports

Visual timelines and annotated maps

Short source sets with guiding questions

Audio/video sources with captions and transcripts

Role cards and structured seminar routines

Vocabulary banks and cause-effect organizers

Alternatives to timed tests

Individualization: Teachers follow each student’s IEP and use present-level data, accommodations, assistive technology, and specially designed instruction. Supports provide access without lowering the intellectual dignity of the work.

Family and community connection

Each semester includes at least one opportunity for students to connect learning to family knowledge, New Orleans, Akan or African diasporic culture, a community concern, or a public audience. Participation options protect privacy and respect different family circumstances.
