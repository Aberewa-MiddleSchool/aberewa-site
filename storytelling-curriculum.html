All-Student Enrichment · Grades 7–8

Storytelling

Voice, listening, memory, craft, performance, and ethical community stories

Students study how stories travel through oral performance, print, image, music, theatre, comics, and digital media. Akan Ananse traditions, African diasporic storytelling, New Orleans voices, family knowledge, contemporary fiction, and student imagination become contexts for craft—always with source attribution, consent, and alternatives to personal disclosure.

Curriculum foundation

Listening is a storytelling skill

Students practice attention, paraphrase, response, turn-taking, and respectful silence. Communication differences, AAC, processing time, and non-speaking participation are treated as part of the storytelling community.

Sources and tellers matter

Students identify who told, recorded, translated, adapted, or published a story and how power, audience, migration, and medium may change it.

No required trauma

Students may create fiction or use public, teacher-vetted sources. Disability, family history, grief, migration, religion, violence, or identity is never demanded as the price of authentic work.

Implementation standard: Oral-history, recording, photography, publication, and cultural adaptation follow written consent and source procedures. Teachers review media for age appropriateness, distinguish Akan or Asante-specific material from broader African or diasporic traditions, and consult culture bearers when context is uncertain.

Enduring learning goals

01

Listen actively and identify sequence, character, setting, conflict, pattern, point of view, theme, and performance choices.

02

Plan, draft, rehearse, revise, and present original oral, written, visual, dramatic, or digital narratives.

03

Use voice, pacing, gesture, repetition, imagery, dialogue, sound, and audience awareness purposefully.

04

Research Akan Ananse stories and African diasporic transformations with accurate source and cultural notes.

05

Conduct or analyze an ethical interview or oral-history process with consent, boundaries, and faithful representation.

06

Curate and publish accessible stories using captions, transcripts, alt text, audio description, plain-language summaries, or AAC.

Grade 7 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Story Circles, Listening & Structure

Essential question: What helps a group listen closely enough for every storyteller to belong?

Core learning: community agreements; turn-taking; AAC access; beginning-middle-end; character wants; conflict; sequence; retell versus interpretation; feedback

Culminating demonstration
Tell or direct a two-to-three-minute original or public-domain story and respond to a listener’s retelling.
9 weeks

Ananse, Tricksters & Sourced Retelling

Essential question: Why do trickster stories entertain, challenge power, and change as they travel?

Core learning: Ananse in Akan and Asante contexts; trickster patterns; humor; consequences; oral variation; translation; diaspora; attribution; adaptation choices

Culminating demonstration
Create a sourced retelling or comparative map that identifies what came from the source and what the student changed.
9 weeks

New Orleans Voices & Place Stories

Essential question: How can a place hold many true, remembered, and imagined stories?

Core learning: neighborhood; soundscape; public history; folklore; point of view; setting detail; maps; primary and secondary sources; fact versus invention

Culminating demonstration
Produce a place story using public sources, observation, or a consented interview and clearly label fact, memory, and fiction.
9 weeks

Performance, Sound & Digital Story

Essential question: How does a medium change the way an audience experiences a story?

Core learning: voice; gesture; pacing; music and sound rights; storyboard; recording; editing; captions; transcript; credits; audience testing

Culminating demonstration
Publish an accessible audio, video, live, visual, or AAC-mediated story with a transcript or equivalent access feature.
Grade 8 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Adaptation, Perspective & Ethics

Essential question: Who has the right to change a story, and what responsibilities follow?

Core learning: adaptation; public domain; copyright awareness; cultural ownership; perspective shift; translator and collector roles; stereotypes; source notes; permission

Culminating demonstration
Write an adaptation proposal and create a short sample that explains permissions, sources, changes, and cultural limits.
9 weeks

Memoir, Fiction & Speculative Futures

Essential question: How do storytellers use truth, invention, and possibility without confusing them?

Core learning: memory and reliability; scene; reflection; character arc; worldbuilding; African and diasporic futures; New Orleans resilience; labels for fiction and nonfiction

Culminating demonstration
Create a polished narrative in a chosen genre with a craft commentary and a non-personal option.
9 weeks

Oral History & Community Knowledge

Essential question: How can an interviewer preserve another person’s meaning without taking ownership of it?

Core learning: research; open questions; informed consent; recording permission; active listening; transcription; verification; editing boundaries; withdrawal; archive choices

Culminating demonstration
Complete a consented interview project or analyze a public oral-history recording using the same ethics checklist.
9 weeks

Story Festival, Archive & Public Audience

Essential question: How can a community share stories while protecting access, credit, and privacy?

Core learning: curation; sequence; hosting; publicity; content notes; accessibility; permissions; metadata; credits; audience feedback; preservation; takedown plan

Culminating demonstration
Contribute an original or authorized work to a class festival or archive and document accessibility, consent, and source decisions.

PracticeStudents rehearse one step at a time with visuals, feedback, and repetition.
ApplyStudents use the skill in an authentic task, simulation, studio, or community context.

Daily practice: Instruction may combine mini-lessons, visuals, movement, music, hands-on materials, role-play, small groups, assistive technology, independent work, and teacher conferencing. Pacing changes when student evidence shows a need for reteaching, more practice, or extension.

Signature learning experiences

Ananse and diaspora pathway

Students trace one story pattern through a clearly sourced Akan or Asante version and one diasporic version, noting continuity, change, collector, translator, and context.

Restorative story circle

Students use fictional or low-risk scenarios to practice listening, naming impact, imagining repair, and keeping private information outside the public performance.

New Orleans sound-and-place story

Students combine public history, field observation, maps, and licensed or original sound to create an accessible place narrative.

Community story festival

Students serve as storytellers, interviewers, editors, performers, access coordinators, hosts, archivists, and consent reviewers.

Assessment without high-stakes testing

Frequent evidence

  • Observation with a task-specific checklist
  • Student explanation, demonstration, or role-play
  • Portfolio artifacts, photographs, audio, or video when consented
  • Brief reflection and goal-setting conferences

Culminating evidence

  • Authentic unit demonstrations and projects
  • Application across more than one setting or format
  • Student self-assessment, revision, and explanation
  • Teacher rubric tied to the stated skill—not disability characteristics

Reporting progress

Progress reports identify what a student can do independently, with a prompt, with a model, or with direct assistance. Reports also name the next teachable step and the supports that improve access.

Reteaching and reassessment

Students receive a new explanation, adjusted materials, additional guided practice, and another reasonable opportunity to demonstrate growth.

Inclusive and IEP-aligned supports

Story maps and sequence tools

Picture cards, timelines, character maps, color-coded structure, sentence frames, and storyboard templates support planning.

Communication choice

Students may speak, sign, use AAC, type, draw, animate, act, select recorded lines, direct a partner, or combine modes.

Rehearsal and predictability

Scripts, model performances, repeated practice, previewed audiences, clear time limits, and opt-in recording reduce performance uncertainty.

Sensory access

Quiet presentation spaces, reduced lighting, headphones, breaks, asynchronous recording, and audience-size choices support regulation.

Language access

Bilingual work, captions, transcripts, vocabulary preview, text-to-speech, speech-to-text, and plain-language summaries broaden participation.

Feedback with boundaries

Rubrics focus on stated craft choices. Peers do not evaluate a student’s disability, accent, family, identity, trauma, or willingness to disclose.

Individualization: Teachers follow each student’s IEP, present levels, accommodations, communication system, behavior or safety plans, assistive-technology needs, and specially designed instruction. Independence is never confused with withholding necessary support.

Consent, source, and story-care safeguards

A powerful story does not cancel the teller’s right to privacy, credit, context, or withdrawal.

Consent is specific and revisitable

Permission to interview is not automatically permission to record, publish, edit, post online, or preserve permanently. Each use is explained and documented.

Credit the chain

Students name the teller, community, collector, translator, adapter, archive, and publication when known and acknowledge uncertainty rather than inventing a clean origin.

Personal disclosure remains optional

Students may use fiction, public oral histories, historical sources, or teacher-created scenarios. No grade depends on sharing trauma, diagnosis, family conflict, religion, or private identity.

Research and instructional reference points

Teachers use these sources as planning references, then adapt instruction to Aberewa students, local requirements, current safety guidance, and the expertise of qualified educators and community partners.

Smithsonian NMAAHC: Storytelling in the African Diaspora

Museum context for shared and changing storytelling traditions across African-descended communities in the Americas.

Smithsonian Folkways: Ashanti Folk Tales from Ghana

An archival collection used critically by naming the recorder, adaptation history, time period, and limitations.

Library of Congress: Comics and Literacy

Examples and educator resources connecting comics, graphic narrative, reading, and diverse audiences.

U.S. Copyright Office: Fair Use FAQ

Current introductory guidance explaining that fair use depends on circumstances rather than a fixed percentage rule.

Family and community connection

Students may invite a willing relative, elder, artist, librarian, culture bearer, or community member to tell a public story or discuss storytelling practice. The contributor chooses whether recording is allowed, reviews the use, receives credit, and may set limits. A public-source option carries equal academic value.

Privacy and choice: Family participation is welcomed but never required for a student to succeed. Students may use teacher-provided examples whenever sharing household, financial, health, cultural, or personal information would be uncomfortable or unsafe.
