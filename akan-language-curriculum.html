All-Student Enrichment · Grades 7–8

Akan Language

Beginner communication, careful listening, cultural context, and respect for language variety

Students develop beginning listening, speaking, reading, writing, and cultural inquiry in a clearly named Akan instructional variety. The course introduces relationships among Asante Twi, Akuapem Twi, Fante, Bono/Brong, and other Akan varieties without pretending that one form represents every Akan-speaking community.

Curriculum foundation

Name the variety

Every lesson identifies the instructional variety and source. A course may focus on Asante Twi, Akuapem Twi, Fante, or another qualified pathway, but differences in pronunciation, vocabulary, spelling, and usage are not marked as student errors without context.

Communication before perfection

Students build understandable, respectful communication through listening, repetition, songs, visuals, gestures, role-play, reading, and supported writing.

Language and culture are living

Akan is taught through present-day speakers, places, media, relationships, and change—not only through proverbs, historical objects, or generalized tradition.

Implementation standard: A qualified Akan-speaking instructor, reviewer, or community language partner verifies pronunciation, orthography, dialect labels, and cultural usage before materials are taught or published. Automated translation and AI output are treated only as drafts to be checked by a competent speaker.

Enduring learning goals

01

Recognize and produce the core sound and writing patterns of the selected Akan instructional variety with appropriate support.

02

Use greetings, introductions, classroom language, courtesy, repair strategies, and help-seeking expressions in short interactions.

03

Understand and communicate about people, numbers, time, routines, food, places, directions, preferences, and community life.

04

Read and compose short, supported texts using familiar vocabulary and increasingly connected sentences.

05

Listen to, retell, interpret, and responsibly contextualize proverbs, songs, and short narratives.

06

Compare Akan varieties and cultural practices with curiosity, evidence, and respect rather than ranking one as the only correct form.

Grade 7 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Language Map, Sounds & Classroom Communication

Essential question: How do careful listening and a shared sound system make communication possible?

Core learning: Akan language map; named instructional variety; vowels including ɛ and ɔ; tone awareness; syllables; greetings; classroom requests; repetition and repair

Culminating demonstration
Complete a listening-and-speaking portfolio with an alphabet or sound guide reviewed by a qualified speaker.
9 weeks

Identity, Family & Community

Essential question: How do language choices show relationship, respect, and belonging?

Core learning: introductions; names; pronouns; kinship vocabulary; descriptions; possessives; courtesy; age and role; privacy; simple questions and answers

Culminating demonstration
Create and perform a fictional introduction dialogue or a consented personal version in speech, AAC-supported speech, or recorded format.
9 weeks

Numbers, Time, School & Market Language

Essential question: How does language help us organize everyday activity and exchange?

Core learning: numbers; days and time; classroom objects; quantities; prices; simple transactions; schedules; question words; negation; visual reading

Culminating demonstration
Operate a simulated classroom market or schedule station using supported Akan phrases, numbers, and written labels.
9 weeks

Proverbs, Songs & Short Narratives

Essential question: How can a short form carry memory, humor, values, and more than one interpretation?

Core learning: oral performance; call and response; proverb context; Ananse awareness; sequence words; repeated structures; literal and inferred meaning; attribution

Culminating demonstration
Present a short, sourced proverb or narrative with a translation, context note, visual support, and explanation of what remains uncertain.
Grade 8 scope and sequence
9 weeks

Daily Life, Food & Well-Being

Essential question: How can we communicate needs, preferences, routines, and boundaries respectfully?

Core learning: daily verbs; food and household vocabulary; likes and dislikes; health and comfort expressions; requesting help; commands and politeness; sentence expansion

Culminating demonstration
Create an accessible bilingual daily-routine or cooking demonstration using teacher-verified language.
9 weeks

Places, Directions & Community Participation

Essential question: How do we ask where, explain movement, and solve a misunderstanding?

Core learning: location; direction; landmarks; transport; prepositions or locative structures; clarification; phone or message scripts; signs; maps

Culminating demonstration
Guide a partner through a fictional map route and successfully repair two planned communication breakdowns.
9 weeks

Description, Comparison & Connected Speech

Essential question: How do speakers move from a phrase to an organized explanation?

Core learning: adjectives and qualities; comparison; reasons; sequence; present, past, and future meaning at an introductory level; paragraph frames; listening notes

Culminating demonstration
Produce a short oral and written description of a person, place, object, artwork, or community practice using sourced vocabulary.
9 weeks

Bilingual Community Portfolio

Essential question: How can beginning language learners contribute accurately and humbly?

Core learning: interview ethics; translation choices; captions; pronunciation revision; dialect notes; glossary design; audience; peer review; reflection

Culminating demonstration
Publish a beginner bilingual portfolio or exhibition reviewed by a qualified speaker and accompanied by a language-variety statement.

Weekly instructional cycle

ConnectActivate prior knowledge, culture, purpose, and the student’s personal goal.
ModelTeacher demonstrates the complete process with clear thinking aloud.
PracticeStudents rehearse one step at a time with visuals, feedback, and repetition.
ApplyStudents use the skill in an authentic task, simulation, studio, or community context.
ReflectStudents document growth, revise work, and choose a practical next step.

Daily practice: Instruction may combine mini-lessons, visuals, movement, music, hands-on materials, role-play, small groups, assistive technology, independent work, and teacher conferencing. Pacing changes when student evidence shows a need for reteaching, more practice, or extension.

Signature learning experiences

Pronunciation and access bank

Students help build captioned audio, symbol, text, and AAC supports for teacher-verified classroom expressions.

Akan variety comparison

Using qualified sources, students compare a small set of words or expressions across named varieties such as Asante Twi, Akuapem Twi, Fante, or Bono/Brong and explain why labels matter.

Proverb context gallery

Students research who uses a proverb, when it may be appropriate, how translations differ, and why a symbol or one-line English gloss cannot carry every meaning.

Bilingual community exhibition

Students combine short dialogues, labels, maps, stories, songs, or cultural research into an accessible public learning display after speaker review.

Assessment without high-stakes testing

Frequent evidence

  • Observation with a task-specific checklist
  • Student explanation, demonstration, or role-play
  • Portfolio artifacts, photographs, audio, or video when consented
  • Brief reflection and goal-setting conferences

Culminating evidence

  • Authentic unit demonstrations and projects
  • Application across more than one setting or format
  • Student self-assessment, revision, and explanation
  • Teacher rubric tied to the stated skill—not disability characteristics

Reporting progress

Progress reports identify what a student can do independently, with a prompt, with a model, or with direct assistance. Reports also name the next teachable step and the supports that improve access.

Reteaching and reassessment

Students receive a new explanation, adjusted materials, additional guided practice, and another reasonable opportunity to demonstrate growth.

Inclusive and IEP-aligned supports

Multimodal input

Teacher speech, qualified-speaker audio, lip visibility, text, images, gestures, objects, captions, and repetition provide multiple paths to meaning.

Low-pressure rehearsal

Private practice, choral response, partner rehearsal, recorded attempts, and extra wait time come before individual public speaking.

AAC and alternative output

Students may use programmed vocabulary, communication boards, eye gaze, typing, signing, partner-assisted scanning, or recorded speech to meet communication goals.

Sound and spelling supports

Color-coded vowel charts, syllable blocks, mouth diagrams, slow audio, playback, and side-by-side dialect notes support perception and production.

Memory supports

Spaced retrieval, songs, rhythm, movement, word walls, personal phrase books, and predictable routines strengthen retention.

Error response with dignity

Teachers recast, model, and invite another attempt without mocking accent, speech disability, processing time, or home-language influence.

Individualization: Teachers follow each student’s IEP, present levels, accommodations, communication system, behavior or safety plans, assistive-technology needs, and specially designed instruction. Independence is never confused with withholding necessary support.

Language accuracy and cultural accountability

Akan language teaching must remain accountable to living speakers and clearly identified varieties.

No unverified publishing

Website text, songs, pronunciation recordings, tattoos, signs, logos, and permanent displays using Akan language or symbols receive review from a qualified speaker or cultural advisor.

Difference is not deficiency

Asante Twi, Akuapem Twi, Fante, Bono/Brong, and other forms are not collapsed into one undifferentiated “African language.” Teachers explain variation and avoid ranking communities.

Consent and privacy

Students do not imitate accents for humor, claim an identity they do not hold, or pressure community members to translate, perform, or share cultural knowledge without consent and credit.

Research and instructional reference points

Teachers use these sources as planning references, then adapt instruction to Aberewa students, local requirements, current safety guidance, and the expertise of qualified educators and community partners.

Ghana NaCCA: Common Core Programme

Official Ghana curriculum portal with Ghanaian Language and related junior-high subject resources.

NaCCA: Ghanaian Language Curriculum

Official curriculum reference for language learning, communication, literacy, cultural identity, and assessment.

University of Ghana Digital Collections

A historical Asante and Fante language reference used critically alongside current speakers and current orthography.

Ghana NaCCA: Curriculum Development

Official explanation of standards-based curriculum, core competencies, assessment, and instructional materials.

Family and community connection

Willing Akan-speaking relatives and community members may review pronunciation, tell a public story, explain variation, or discuss when an expression is appropriate. They are credited for their expertise and may decline recording. Students without an Akan-speaking family connection use the same high-quality school resources.

Privacy and choice: Family participation is welcomed but never required for a student to succeed. Students may use teacher-provided examples whenever sharing household, financial, health, cultural, or personal information would be uncomfortable or unsafe.
